  • Selene Veyra protect caravan
    • Race/Class: Half-Elf Rogue 5
    • Profession: Head of the Silverstream Traders’ Guild, overseeing caravans, trade, and silver exports.
    • Personality:
      • Charismatic: Warm smile, quick to laugh, generous in taverns.
      • Calculating: Behind the charm, every favor she grants ties someone subtly into her web.
    • Public Persona:

      Selene is renowned for her charm and wit. She is a deft negotiator, always able to smooth tensions between traders, miners, and farmers. Many in Willowford admire her fairness, generosity, and ability to bring wealth to the community. Children know her as the guildmistress who hands out sweets during festivals, while the council values her skill at securing favorable trade contracts.

    • Secret Life:

      Beneath her polished image, Selene orchestrates a discreet drug smuggling network. Using her control of silver shipments, she conceals and exports powdered spores derived from corrupted fungi found in sealed caverns beneath the Silverstream Hills. The substance, known as “Hollowdust” among its users, induces euphoria and heightened sensitivity but is dangerously addictive, eroding body and mind over time.

    • Operation:
      • The powder is pressed into false seams within silver ingots or hidden in grain sacks shipped downriver.
      • A small circle of trusted smugglers and ferrymen carry out the work, loyal to Selene either through coin or fear of exposure.
      • She maintains plausible deniability, ensuring she is never seen handling Hollowdust directly.
      • Selene Veyra’s smugglers secretly cultivate the fungi in a cavern away from the worst infestations.
      • Harvesters wear damp cloth masks and protective charms against the spirit’s corruption.
      • Even then, many of them bear fungal scars or chronic coughs.

  • Old Toma Reed
    • Race/Class: Human Wizard 6 (Divination, retired)
      • forage in forest
    • Profession: Village scholar who studies the magical echoes of the Old Willow Tree and its spirits.
    • Studies:

      The Old Willow Tree is not just a shrine — it’s a resting place for many lesser spirits, and over centuries, their presence has left resonant imprints in the world. Toma believes these are what he calls “magical echoes.”

      • Spirit Residue: When villagers pray, offer gifts, or celebrate at the tree, faint traces of spirit energy linger. These impressions accumulate like layers of incense smoke over generations.
      • Whispers of the Past: On still nights, Toma claims to hear faint voices or feel visions of past festivals and offerings. These aren’t ghosts, but “memories” left by the spirits.
      • Spell Resonance: Certain spells (divination, augury, commune) cast near the tree are unusually potent or produce unusual side effects — glimpses of long-dead druids, voices of forgotten ancestors, flashes of natural cycles yet to come.
      • Aura of Fertility: The soil around the tree is unnaturally rich. Even plants near it grow strong, and small animals frequently linger there, seemingly unafraid.
      • Mystical Harmonics: Toma describes the echoes as a “humming undercurrent,” like a string plucked but never fading. He theorizes it may be the collective breath of countless spirits tied to Willowford’s land.

      For Toma, studying these echoes means learning how spirit energy persists and communicates with mortals. He hopes one day to distill that essence into protective wards or divination tools.

Travellers

  • Barnabas
    • Race/Class: Tabaxi Bard
    • Profession: Traveling storyteller, poet, and collector of tales
    • Description: Barnabas is a cheetah-furred tabaxi with bright, alert eyes and an ever-present theatrical smile. He wears a fine bright blue coat over a billowy white shirt, brown trousers cut at three-quarter length, and goes barefoot regardless of terrain. A wide-brimmed hat matching his coat, adorned with a sweeping plume, marks him instantly in any crowd—stylish but more artistic than ostentatious.
    • Personality: Flowery and expressive in speech, Barnabas layers conversations with compliments, metaphors, and dramatic flourishes. He treats nearly everyone as a potential hero of a grand tale and delights in elevating ordinary deeds into legend.
    • Habits: Travels constantly between towns, cities, and encampments performing poems and stories. When he encounters adventurers or interesting travelers, he eagerly offers coin in exchange for their songs, personal histories, or written verses, carefully preserving them in a growing collection.
    • Backstory: Barnabas escaped slavery years ago, stowing away aboard a merchant vessel that eventually reached Veythar. Since gaining his freedom, he has wandered the land gathering stories as proof that lives can be rewritten. Though other tabaxi travel the northern regions, he has never journeyed there.

    “The Ballad of Old Harl and the Uncooperative Swine”

    Oh gather close and lend an ear,

    To tragedy both grand and queer—

    Not war, nor dragon, ghost, nor king,

    But pigs… and what those pigs did bring.

    Old Harl awoke at break of day,

    With straw in beard and debts to pay,

    He stretched, he yawned, he scratched his knee—

    Then shrieked loud enough to wake the sea.

    “My pigs!” he cried, “My pride! My stock!”

    The pen stood empty. Gate unlocked.

    No hoofprint trail, no muddy sign,

    Just silence… and one missing twine.

    He searched the fields, he searched the brook,

    He checked each place a pig might look,

    Behind the barn, beneath a cart—

    Each failure breaking Harl’s poor heart.

    He asked the baker, stern and wide,

    “Seen pigs?” — “No pigs,” the baker replied.

    He asked the smith with soot-black grin,

    “Only sausages,” said he, “come in.”

    Through town he ran in wild despair,

    With bits of hay still in his hair,

    Accusing ducks and questioning goats,

    Interrogating fence posts’ notes.

    At last he found them, calm and pleased,

    In Widow May’s prize carrot trees—

    Six pigs reclining, round and pink,

    Quite satisfied, I’m led to think.

    They’d tunneled out beneath the fence,

    A feat of porcine excellence,

    For Harl, it seemed, had failed to note:

    Pigs plan more than farmers quote.

    So raise a mug to Harl’s hard day,

    And pigs who simply walked away—

    For beasts may grunt and roll in muck,

    But never underestimate livestock luck!

    And should your dinner squeal too loud,

    Or wander off both free and proud—

    Remember well this farmer’s cry:

    “Count your pigs… and count them twice!”
