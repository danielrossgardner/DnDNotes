Thar Du Rak

Thar Du Rak — Land of Blood

Thar Du Rak sits to the east of Veythar across open water, its name drawn from an ancient founding oath — blood and salt mixed together as a covenant between its people and the sea that shapes them. It is a land that has never been fully tamed, and its people have never wished it to be.

People & Culture

The half-orcs of Thar Du Rak are a maritime people to their core. Society mirrors the structure of a great sailing vessel — elders serve as captains, families are organized as crews, and hierarchy, discipline, and clear orders are not impositions but the natural language of a culture that learned long ago what happens when a ship has no chain of command.

Those in active service — sailors and marines — wear crisp white uniforms, instantly recognizable in any port on any sea. Layfolk wear shades of blue, marking their connection to the sea while distinguishing them from those currently serving. Physical competition is woven into daily life: wrestling, rowing races, stone-lifting, and sparring earn prestige and the right to wear emblems on one's uniform, each one a public record of what a person has proven.

Sea spirits are revered above all — particularly those of winds, currents, and storm fronts. Offerings are made before every voyage without exception. Many sailors forgo shrine offerings entirely in favor of tattooing wave motifs directly onto their skin, carrying their devotion with them onto the water where it belongs.

The Land

Thar Du Rak's western coast faces Veythar across open water and is defined by low, flat terrain — wide tidal flats, marshland, and coastal plains that stretch inland in an unbroken green expanse. The sky here is enormous and the wind off the water is constant. Settlements cluster along the shore and river mouths, built low and sturdy against the weather, their docks extending into grey water like reaching hands.

Moving east the land rises gradually and the open plains give way to vast ancient forests — dense, dark-canopied, and old enough that the trees have names in the oldest half-orc songs. The forests are neither tamed nor entirely wild. Logging roads and hunting trails cut through them, and small inland settlements dot the clearings, but the deep forest remains largely uncharted and commands a quiet respect from those who live at its edge.

Further east still the land rises sharply into a jagged mountain range — steep, unforgiving, and dangerous in all seasons. The peaks are not merely difficult to cross but actively hostile: rockfall, sudden storms, and terrain that defeats even experienced climbers. No road crosses them. No trade route has ever been established through them. The half-orcs of Thar Du Rak are a people who have mastered the sea in every direction and built a civilization on that mastery — but the mountains at their back remain an absolute boundary. What lies beyond them is not spoken of with curiosity. It is spoken of, when it is spoken of at all, with the particular unease reserved for things that have no answer.
